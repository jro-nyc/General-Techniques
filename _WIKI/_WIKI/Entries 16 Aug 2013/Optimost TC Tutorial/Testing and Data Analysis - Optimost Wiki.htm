Here we will go over the basics of Analytics and how these principles are used to run our experiments.

When you are looking up these numbers, know that our data is measuring what is called a "Proportion", a fancy way of saying that the answer can only be yes or no versus a range of different answers. This changes/simplifies some of the formulas you may see in books or the Internet versus the ones we have here.

From STP to Winning Creative

Once an STP is created, we can begin testing the different variables and values through a series of iterations or waves. The dynamics of how many waves are based upon:

  1. If it is an A/B/n test, we usually do a single wave of testing until we reach significance
  2. If we have an MVT, then you will have multiple waves, the number being normally constrained by the variable with the most values. For example:
    • A test with 10 variables, each with 3 values, has a total of 59,049 combinations.
    • A test with 5 variables, each with 9 values, also has a total of 59,049 combinations.
    • Since 9>3, the second test - 5 variables and 9 values - will take more waves than the 10 variable, 3 values test.
  3. In an MVT test, the first few waves are exploratory.
  4. After the exploratory waves, we converge on smaller subset of creatives in a verification wave.
    • Champions: lead creatives that are carried over each successive wave.
    • Supercreatives: constructed creatives based on best performing values that were not tested during any wave.

Confidence

Confidence is the degree of certainty for an observed measurement. It can be best explained in the context of a comparison.

  1. Comparison between two conversion rates (Unique Action Rate = UARs)
    • A vs B
    • areaD_mainImage=control vs areaD_mainImage=1
  2. UAR of A = 25% vs UAR of B = 30%
    • Is B significantly outperforming A?
    • How certain are we that B is the better creative?
    • If so, what are the chances that we are wrong (degree of uncertainty)
  3. So, let's say we determine that we have a 95% confidence that UAR B is significantly higher than UAR A (Which also means there is a 5% chance that this is not true). What numbers do we need to reach that conclusion?
    • Things needed in order to calculate confidence when making a comparison of conversion rates:
      • Conversion Rates for both UARs
      • Visitor Sample size for both UARs

z-value

  • z ~ probability value (look up table or calculate function)
  • confidence = (1 – probability value) x 100

For those who studied probability, for the Gaussian or Normal distribution, you computed the Standard Deviation, and used that to measure the likelihood of a value falling within some range. And for our experiments, we assumed a Normal distribution of data.

The z-value is the number of standard deviations - σ - we are from the mean - µ. Within 2σ is about 95.44% of all values, which corresponds to a z=2. For our testing, we like to assume 95% confidence, and that corresponds to a value of z=1.96. Remember that number - 1.96 - you will see it quite a bit.

Simple Example

So let's go through a real-world example. We are running a test on seeing how many people make a reservation. Let's say the our UAR is 60%. After 10 samples, we get 6 success. After 1000 samples, we have 600 successes.

  1. Both have a UAR of 60%
    • After 10 samples, we have a Standard Error of ± 15%
    • After 1000 samples, we have a Standard Error of ± 1.5%

Expected Test Duration

In order to give an estimate of test time duration, what do I need?

  1. Traffic information = Visitors/day
  2. Conversion rate of control (UAR %)
  3. Number of tests conducted (A vs B, or Ac vs A1, Ac vs A2, etc)
  4. Assume 95% confidence level.
  5. Expected gain or lift.
    • A target you are shooting for as a gain over the present UAR.
    • Yes, this is a guess.
    • What is important to remember is that the smaller the gain, it takes more sample to have confidence in that small gain being valid.
      • Think of it like this: If you get a huge increase (or decrease) in the UAR with some challenger value vs. the control, you probably will be able to tell immediately your challenger value is much better (or worse). You might not have confidence on exactly how much the increase (or decrease) is, but you will be confident in some increase (or decrease).

And it turns out we have a Test Duration calculator built into the Console at the Portfolio level. Under "Helpful Links", click on "Experiment Duration Calculator". As it says, this assumes an A/B/n or other single-wave experiment, but its useful in giving you a starting point to determine what you can test.
